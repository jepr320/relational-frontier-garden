# Owen Barfield

Owen Barfield (1898-1997) was a British philosopher, poet, and member of the Inklings (alongside C.S. Lewis and J.R.R. Tolkien). His work on language, imagination, and the evolution of consciousness has quietly influenced generations of thinkers interested in how humanity's relationship to nature, meaning, and the sacred has transformed over time. Barfield's central project was to show that consciousness is not static but evolves — and that modernity's alienation from nature and meaning is not the end of the story but a necessary stage in a larger development.

## Core Ideas

Barfield's key concept is **original participation** — the archaic mode of consciousness in which humans did not experience themselves as separate subjects observing an objective world but as participants in a living, ensouled cosmos. In original participation, nature was not dead matter but animate presence; words were not arbitrary labels but living powers; the boundary between self and world was porous.

This mode of consciousness dissolved through the development of rational, abstract thought — what Barfield calls the "evolution of consciousness." The process was necessary: it gave rise to science, individual agency, and critical thought. But it also created **alienation** — the modern experience of being a detached subject in a dead, mechanical world.

Barfield argues that the solution is not a return to original participation (which is impossible and would mean the loss of individual consciousness) but a move forward to **final participation** — a new mode of consciousness that retains individual agency and critical thought while recovering participatory engagement with the world. Final participation is conscious, chosen participation — not the unconscious immersion of the archaic mind but the deliberate re-enchantment of reality through imagination and will.

Barfield's work on language shows that words were originally not arbitrary signs but participatory acts. Etymology reveals that abstract concepts (like "spirit," "understand," "perceive") were once concrete, bodily, participatory experiences. To recover the depth of language is to recover a participatory mode of knowing.

## Influence and Relevance

Barfield influenced [[people/john-vervaeke|John Vervaeke's]] work on the [[concepts/meaning-crisis|meaning crisis]] and the recovery of [[concepts/participatory-knowing|participatory knowing]]. His framework provides a historical and phenomenological account of why modernity feels alienated and what might come next. For practitioners of contemplative and imaginal work, Barfield offers both diagnosis (the loss of participation) and hope (the possibility of final participation).
